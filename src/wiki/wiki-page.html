Building a Swale System for Water Retention

Sarah Chen November 7, 2025 15 min read 127 views
Water Management Earthworks Permaculture Design
Edit This Page

Swales are one of the most powerful water harvesting techniques in permaculture. These on-contour ditches with berms capture, slow, and infiltrate runoff water into the landscape, recharging groundwater and supporting deep-rooted vegetation.

What is a Swale?

A swale is a level ditch dug on contour (following the land's natural level lines) with a raised berm on the downhill side. Unlike ditches designed to move water away, swales are designed to slow water down and allow it to infiltrate into the soil. This makes them invaluable in:

  • Drought-prone areas for water retention
  • Sloped land to prevent erosion
  • Food forests and orchards for deep watering
  • Regenerating degraded landscapes

Site Assessment

Before building any swales, you need to understand your landscape. Here's what to assess:

1. Contour Mapping

Use an A-frame level, laser level, or surveying equipment to map contour lines. Swales must be perfectly level along their length to prevent water from flowing to one end and breaking through.

2. Water Flow Patterns

Observe where water flows during rain events. Look for:

  • Existing erosion channels
  • Areas of water accumulation
  • Fast-flowing runoff paths
  • Soil moisture patterns after rain

3. Soil Type and Infiltration Rate

Different soils absorb water at different rates. Perform a simple percolation test:

  1. Dig a hole 30cm deep and wide
  2. Fill it with water and let it drain completely
  3. Fill it again and measure how long it takes to drain
  4. Ideal infiltration: 5-15 cm per hour
"I spent a full month just observing water patterns on our property before we dug a single swale. That observation saved us from making costly mistakes and helped us place swales exactly where they'd be most effective." - Sarah Chen

Design Principles

Swale Dimensions

Size your swales based on your rainfall, slope, and catchment area:

  • Width: Typically 1-3 meters at the top
  • Depth: 0.5-1 meter depending on scale
  • Berm height: 0.3-0.6 meters above grade
  • Length: As long as possible while staying on contour
  • Spacing: 10-30 meters apart vertically, depending on slope

Overflow Planning

Every swale needs planned overflow points for extreme rain events. Design spillways that:

  • Are armored with rocks or vegetation to prevent erosion
  • Connect to the next swale downslope when possible
  • Spread water rather than concentrating flow

Construction Steps

Step 1: Mark Your Contour Lines

Using your A-frame or level, mark the contour with stakes and string. This line should be perfectly level - any variation will cause problems.

Step 2: Excavate the Ditch

You can dig by hand for small swales or use machinery for larger systems. Keep the following in mind:

  • Store topsoil separately to replace on the berm
  • Create a flat bottom, not V-shaped
  • Maintain consistent depth along the entire length
  • Slope the sides gently (3:1 ratio) for stability

Step 3: Build the Berm

Place excavated soil on the downhill side to create the berm:

  1. Compact subsoil layers as you build up
  2. Top with saved topsoil
  3. Shape with gentle slopes to prevent erosion
  4. Extend the berm past each end of the swale
üì∑ Image: Excavator digging swale with berm formation

Step 4: Plant the System

Vegetation stabilizes your swale and maximizes its benefits:

  • On the berm: Deep-rooted trees and shrubs (fruit trees, nitrogen-fixers)
  • Along swale edges: Vetiver grass or other erosion control plants
  • In the swale bed: Initially, leave bare to maximize infiltration, then plant after first season

Maintenance

Swales are relatively low-maintenance, but they do need attention:

  • Check for erosion after major rain events
  • Remove sediment buildup annually
  • Repair any breaches immediately
  • Trim vegetation to prevent root systems from compromising the berm
  • Add mulch to swale bottoms to improve infiltration

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Not staying on contour: Even a 1% grade will cause water to flow to one end
  • Building too steep of sides: Will erode and collapse over time
  • No overflow planning: Leads to catastrophic washouts
  • Swales too close together: Can saturate soil and cause landslides on steep slopes
  • Ignoring soil type: Clay soils need wider, shallower swales

Expected Results

When properly designed and installed, swales deliver impressive benefits:

  • First year: Visible increase in vegetation health within 10m of swale
  • 2-3 years: Groundwater levels rise, springs may emerge downslope
  • 5+ years: Dramatic landscape transformation with lush vegetation
  • Long term: Soil building, erosion reversal, microclimate improvement
